About Me

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Economics at the University of California, Irvine (expected 2027). My research focuses on empirical industrial organization, with applications in transportation, energy, and consumer goods markets. I use structural econometric models, causal inference methods, and large-scale data analysis to study competition, mergers, and public policy.

My job market paper studies how multimarket contact facilitates tacit coordination on both pricing and capacity decisions in the U.S. airline industry. I also study merger effects in the sports drink and airline industries, electric vehicle adoption, and the macroeconomic implications of AI for labor markets.

Prior to my doctoral studies, I earned an M.A. from Stanford University and a B.A. (Magna Cum Laude, Phi Beta Kappa) from UC Irvine. I have professional experience at the California Air Resources Board and have helped secure over $450k in competitive research funding.

"Competition and Consumer Welfare in Airline Markets: Evidence from the Alaska–Hawaiian Merger" R&R
Chen Zhang
Revised and resubmitted to the Journal of Air Transport Management, 2026
Uses a difference-in-difference method for demand estimation, then develops a two-stage decision-making model considering price and flight frequency as two choice variables by modifying Berry–Levinsohn–Pakes (BLP) framework. "Beyond Fares: Multimarket Contact and Capacity Discipline in Airline Markets" JOB MARKET PAPER
Chen Zhang
This paper investigates whether multimarket contact (MMC) facilitates tacit coordination on both price and non-price margins (flight frequency), and whether such conduct varies across firm types in the airline industry. To address the endogeneity of MMC to market entry and exit, this paper estimates a joint structural model of demand, fares, and schedules, instrumenting for MMC using predetermined carrier access to airport gates. Reduced-form estimates indicate that MMC increases both fares and nonstop flight frequency, disproportionately affecting price-sensitive travelers. Structural estimates demonstrate significant heterogeneity in mutual forbearance: legacy carriers' conduct weights increase from near-zero to approximately 0.4 as MMC rises, whereas low-cost carriers consistently price as Nash–Bertrand competitors. Counterfactual simulations indicate that eliminating MMC-induced coordination would increase annualized consumer surplus by roughly 7.8% and total welfare by 3.5%.
"AI and the Labor Bubble: A General Equilibrium Theory of the Rise and Fall of Mass Employment"
Jiawei Chen and Chen Zhang — February 2026 (Preliminary and Incomplete)
We develop a general equilibrium model in which heterogeneous agents sort into three occupations based on comparative advantage: entrepreneur, worker, or transfer recipient. The capital intensity parameter indexes the state of production technology. We show that the equilibrium worker share traces an inverted-U as capital intensity rises: at low levels, limited returns to scale suppress demand for hired labor; at intermediate levels, capital–labor complementarity creates mass employment; as capital intensity approaches one, labor becomes dispensable and the worker share collapses. We call this arc the labor bubble—mass wage employment as a historically contingent phenomenon.
"Merging to Challenge the Market Leader: An Empirical Analysis in the Sports Drink Industry"
Chen Zhang
Develops causal inference models using difference-in-differences and synthetic control methods to assess the impact of Coca-Cola's $5.6B acquisition of BodyArmor. Models pricing dynamics on 290M+ retail observations across 775 products and 47 brands, quantifying post-merger price shifts (+20% Powerade, +4% BodyArmor) and market concentration changes (+28 HHI points).
